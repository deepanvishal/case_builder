## 1. Statutory / regulatory basis
- INA § 203(b)(1)(A) — immigrant classification for aliens of extraordinary ability.
- 8 CFR § 204.5(h)(2) — "extraordinary ability" = a level of expertise indicating the
  individual is one of that small percentage who have risen to the very top of the
  field of endeavor.
- 8 CFR § 204.5(h)(3) — meet a one-time major internationally recognized award, OR at
  least 3 of 10 regulatory criteria. Criteria at issue here:
  - (v) original scientific/scholarly/business contributions of major significance.
  - (viii) leading or critical role for organizations/establishments with a
    distinguished reputation.
  - (ix) high salary or significantly high remuneration relative to others in the field.
- USCIS Policy Manual, Vol. 6, Part F, Ch. 2 — codifies the two-step analysis and
  per-criterion guidance, including the final-merits determination.

## 2. The two-step analysis — Kazarian v. USCIS, 596 F.3d 1115 (9th Cir. 2010)
- STEP 1 (antecedent / criteria-counting): determine whether the petitioner submitted
  evidence meeting at least 3 of the 10 criteria. At this step USCIS may NOT impose
  requirements beyond the plain language of the regulation (e.g. it may not demand
  field-wide "significance" merely to count a criterion).
- STEP 2 (final merits): weigh ALL the evidence together to decide whether the record
  establishes sustained national/international acclaim and that the petitioner is among
  the small percentage at the very top of the field. Questions of impact, significance,
  and overall standing are resolved HERE, in totality — not by counting.

## 3. Standard of proof — Matter of Chawathe, 25 I&N Dec. 369 (AAO 2010)
- Preponderance of the evidence: the petitioner must show each claim is "more likely
  than not" / "probably true." Even with some doubt, relevant, probative, and credible
  evidence that makes the claim more likely than not satisfies the standard. The Final
  Merits argument is made expressly under this standard.

## 4. "Sustained" acclaim
- Acclaim must be maintained, not a single past peak. Past achievement without
  continued comparable acclaim does not satisfy the standard. Date the evidence and
  show the acclaim is ongoing.

## 6. Per-criterion legal bar
- (v) OCMS: original contribution + major significance IN THE FIELD as a whole.
  Significance is shown by field-level impact, independent corroboration, and adoption/
  replication beyond the employer — not by internal value, conclusory praise, or
  uncorroborated letters (Visinscaia; Amin; Strategati; Caron; Soffici).
- (viii) Leading/critical role: a leading or critical role AND the organization (or a
  distinguished division of it) has a distinguished reputation shown by objective
  evidence (rankings, market position, awards) — not merely the type of work it does.
- (ix) High salary: significantly high remuneration relative to OTHERS AT THE TOP of
  the same occupation and locale, using objective same-field data — not averages, not a
  different occupation, not job-ad ranges (Price; Grimson; Muni; Strategati).

## 6a. Field of endeavor vs. occupational wage peer (reconciliation)
The field of endeavor for extraordinary ability is the narrow specialization (production-grade XAI for regulated healthcare decision-support); it is used for OCMS significance and the Final Merits "top small percentage." The High Salary comparison uses the broader recognized OCCUPATION (SOC 15-2051, Lead/Senior/Principal Data Scientists). This is not inconsistent, and the response must state why wherever both scopes appear:
- Matter of Price requires objective earnings data for those performing "similar work"; published government wage data exists only at the occupational level, and USCIS rejected non-objective/crowdsourced figures. The occupation is the proper, data-supported comparator.
- The occupational benchmark is the more CONSERVATIVE test, not a broadening of the field: the petitioner is measured against the entire, larger occupational pool rather than the narrow niche. Clearing the top tier of that broader pool is harder, and is strong evidence of top standing in the narrower specialization within it.
- The salary peer group is an occupational proxy for the wage element only; it never redefines the field of endeavor. Industry convergence (multiple payers adopting matching) is later validation of the specialization, not evidence the field is broad.
Hold the field narrow for OCMS/Final Merits; benchmark the occupation (conservatively) for salary; state the distinction explicitly so it cannot be read as having-it-both-ways.

## 7. Final merits determination
- Meeting 3 criteria is necessary but NOT sufficient. Under Kazarian step two and the
  Chawathe standard, argue that the totality of the record — the criteria plus accepted
  criteria, woven together — establishes sustained acclaim and that the petitioner is
  among the small percentage at the very top of the field. Show reinforcement across
  criteria; do not present a checklist.
